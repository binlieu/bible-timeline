Exodus & Wilderness

The Exodus Route

Exodus 12–19; Numbers 33; Deuteronomy 34

Israel's deliverance from Egypt: from Rameses through the sea, south to Mount Sinai, north to Kadesh-barnea, and after forty years around Edom to the plains of Moab.

Rameses (Qantir) -> Succoth -> The sea crossing -> Marah -> Elim -> Rephidim (Wadi Feiran) -> Mount Sinai (Jebel Musa) -> Kadesh-barnea -> Ezion-geber -> Plains of Moab -> Mount Nebo

  • Traditional southern route (approx. 1446–1406 BC)

Dotted segments mark disputed locations or uncertain segments.

About this map

Heavily debated. The crossing site is disputed (proposals range from the Bitter Lakes region to the Gulf of Suez; a minority argue for the Gulf of Aqaba). Mount Sinai's identification with Jebel Musa is traditional (Jebel al-Lawz and other candidates have scholarly advocates). Kadesh-barnea's identification with Ein el-Qudeirat is a widely held scholarly opinion. Dates follow the traditional early chronology (Exodus approx. 1446 BC).

Description

Numbers 33 preserves the itinerary of the Exodus in station lists, though most stations cannot be located with certainty. The route shown is the traditional southern route: out of the eastern Nile delta, across the sea, down the west coast of the Sinai peninsula to Jebel Musa (the traditional Mount Sinai), north to Kadesh-barnea where the spies rebelled, and finally — after the wilderness years — around Edom by way of Ezion-geber to the plains of Moab opposite Jericho, where Moses viewed the land from Mount Nebo.
