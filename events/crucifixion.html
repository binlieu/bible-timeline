Summary

Jesus is crucified at Golgotha under Pontius Pilate. The Gospels present his death as the climactic act of obedient suffering, atonement, and kingship.

Jesus is crucified at Golgotha under Pontius Pilate. The Gospels present his death as the climactic act of obedient suffering, atonement, and kingship.

The biblical account (Matthew 27:27-56; John 19) gives the controlling details for crucifixion of jesus. The event is recorded as part of Life of Jesus, and its people, place, and sequence are linked here to show how the narrative develops without adding speculative detail.

This record emphasizes the event's role in the larger biblical timeline. It connects promise, fulfillment, witness, suffering, mission, and hope as the story moves from the ministry of Jesus into the spread of the early church.

Historical Background

Archaeological evidence: The Pilate stone from Caesarea confirms Pontius Pilate as Roman prefect of Judea, and the Caiaphas ossuary is widely discussed as evidence for the high-priestly family known from the passion narratives. Scholarly opinion: The crucifixion is commonly dated either to AD 30 or AD 33, with arguments based on Passover chronology and Gospel sequence; this timeline uses AD 30 for sorting while preserving the divided scholarly date. Historical reconstruction: Roman crucifixion outside Jerusalem fits the political charge and the Gospel presentation of execution under Pilate.
