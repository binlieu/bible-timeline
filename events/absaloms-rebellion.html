Summary

Absalom steals the hearts of Israel and launches a rebellion against David. David flees Jerusalem, Absalom is killed, and the king grieves over his son.

Absalom builds public support through calculated displays of concern for justice, then declares himself king at Hebron. David leaves Jerusalem to avoid immediate bloodshed, and the conflict becomes a national crisis.

Joab's forces defeat Absalom's army, and Joab kills Absalom despite David's command to deal gently with him. The rebellion shows the bitter consequences within David's household and the fragility of royal power.

Historical Background

Historical reconstruction: Palace succession struggles were common in ancient monarchies, and the narrative presents Absalom's revolt in those terms while tying it to David's earlier sin. Scholarly opinion: The account preserves political realism alongside theological interpretation.
