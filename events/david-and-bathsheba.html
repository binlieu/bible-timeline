Summary

David takes Bathsheba and arranges Uriah's death, but Nathan confronts him with his sin. David repents, yet the consequences bring turmoil into his house.

While his army is at war, David abuses royal power by taking Bathsheba and then concealing the pregnancy through the arranged death of Uriah. Nathan exposes the sin through a parable and announces judgment within David's family.

David confesses, and the Lord forgives him, but the narrative does not minimize the harm done. Solomon is later born to David and Bathsheba, keeping the royal line moving through grace after judgment.

Historical Background

Historical reconstruction: The account is unusually candid royal historiography, preserving a severe critique of Israel's most important king. Scholarly opinion: The passage explains later family violence in David's house as moral consequence, not mere court intrigue.
