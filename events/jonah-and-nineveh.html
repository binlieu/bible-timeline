Summary

Jonah is sent to Nineveh, flees, and is brought back to his mission through divine discipline. Nineveh repents at his warning, and God exposes Jonah's resentment of mercy.

Jonah attempts to run from the Lord by sea, but the storm, the great fish, and the renewed commission bring him to Nineveh. His brief warning leads the city to humble itself, and God relents from the announced disaster.

The book presses Israel to recognize the wideness of divine mercy. Jonah's anger over Nineveh's repentance reveals how difficult grace can be when it is extended to enemies.

Historical Background

Historical reconstruction: Nineveh was a major Assyrian city, though it became imperial capital later under Sennacherib. Scholarly opinion: The book uses historical setting and prophetic satire to teach God's compassion toward nations.
