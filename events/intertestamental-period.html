Summary

After Malachi, the biblical timeline enters the period between the Old and New Testaments. Persian, Hellenistic, Maccabean, and Roman rule reshape the world into which the New Testament opens.

This period is historical reconstruction rather than a narrative section of the Protestant Old Testament canon. Judah moves from Persian rule into the Hellenistic age after Alexander the Great, then through Ptolemaic and Seleucid control, the Maccabean revolt, Hasmonean rule, and eventually Roman dominance.

Major developments include the spread of Greek language and culture, the Septuagint translation, the growth of synagogue life, and the rise of groups such as Pharisees and Sadducees. These developments form the historical background for the Gospels without adding new canonical events between Malachi and the New Testament opening.

Historical Background

Historical reconstruction: The period includes Persian, Hellenistic, Maccabean, Hasmonean, and Roman phases before the birth narratives. Scholarly opinion: Daniel 8 and 11 are often discussed in relation to later Hellenistic conflicts, but this event is framed as historical background rather than direct narrative record.
