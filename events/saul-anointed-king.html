Summary

Israel asks Samuel for a king, and God directs him to anoint Saul of Benjamin. Saul is publicly presented at Mizpah as Israel moves from judge-led leadership into monarchy.

Israel asks for a king like the surrounding nations, and Samuel warns them about the burdens royal rule will bring. God tells Samuel to heed the request while making clear that Israel is rejecting divine kingship, not merely Samuel's leadership.

Saul, a Benjamite, is privately anointed and then publicly chosen by lot before the tribes. The event begins Israel's united monarchy and sets the tension that runs through 1 Samuel: the king must rule under the word of the Lord rather than imitate the power politics of the nations.

Historical Background

Historical reconstruction: The rise of monarchy in Israel fits the Iron Age pressure of Philistine military power and the need for centralized defense. Scholarly opinion: Dates for Saul are approximate and are usually reconstructed from the later regnal chronology of David and Solomon.
