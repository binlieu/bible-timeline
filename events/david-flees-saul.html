Summary

Saul grows jealous of David and repeatedly seeks his life. David lives as a fugitive, spares Saul when he has opportunity, and waits for God to give the kingdom.

David's success, covenant friendship with Jonathan, and public acclaim intensify Saul's hostility. The chapters trace David through court danger, wilderness hiding places, Philistine territory, and repeated moral tests.

David refuses to seize the throne by killing Saul, even when Saul is vulnerable at En-gedi. The long fugitive period forms David as a leader and contrasts his restraint with Saul's fear-driven rule.

Historical Background

Historical reconstruction: Wilderness strongholds around En-gedi provided natural refuge near the Dead Sea. Scholarly opinion: The narratives preserve court, clan, and frontier memories from the period before David consolidated rule.
