Summary

Babylon destroys Jerusalem and the temple and exiles many people of Judah. The fall marks the end of the Davidic monarchy as a ruling institution in the land.

After rebellion against Babylon, Jerusalem is besieged, breached, and burned. The temple, palace, and city walls are destroyed, and leading survivors are taken into exile.

The event is the great covenant catastrophe of the Old Testament historical books. It confirms prophetic warnings while creating the setting for exile hope, restoration promises, and the longing for renewed Davidic rule.

Historical Background

Archaeological evidence: Burn layers and destruction remains in Jerusalem correspond to the Babylonian destruction horizon. Historical reconstruction: The fall of Jerusalem is dated to 586 BC, with Babylonian imperial activity also reflected in external chronicle evidence.
